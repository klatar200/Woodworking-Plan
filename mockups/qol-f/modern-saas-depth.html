A vs B is the real decision, and it is an architecture one.
A · Restrained — a 4px lift and a shadow step, pure CSS. Costs nothing, ships no JavaScript, and every card stays a server component.
B · Expressive — pointer-tracked tilt plus a parallax drift on the image. It needs a client island wrapping the catalog grid, i.e. JS on every catalog render, on a page whose whole architecture is currently server-rendered GET forms and links. It also does nothing on touch, which is most of this audience.
My recommendation is A, with B's press feedback (below) kept — the tilt buys the least and costs the most. Both respect reduced motion; toggle it above.

3 · Micro-animations

Click the bookmarks above, press the buttons, switch the tabs.

Three, and only three. Press feedback — a 1px settle and a shadow collapse on :active. Two declarations, no JS, and the cheapest "this feels built" signal there is. Save pop — a 320ms scale on the bookmark, so a tap that writes to the database visibly lands. Tab underline slide — the Sprint 20 tabs already move; this makes the movement legible. The card settle-in (a 6px rise, staggered) replaces today's skeleton→content jump.
Deliberately not proposed: page transitions (they fight client-side routing and delay content), anything parallax on scroll, and any motion on the print or step-walker surfaces — BUILD_PLAN.md §4.4 excludes print from visual work, and the walker is read mid-cut at arm's length.
What a rollout would actually touch, if you approve. plan-card.tsx (depth + hover), src/lib/ui.ts (the button press states, one shared constant), globals.css (three shadow tokens per theme), the catalog page (hero), and the loading skeletons (settle-in instead of a jump). Same pixel-parity discipline as Sprints 28–32: compile every class against the real Tailwind toolchain and diff. No no-JS contract changes — everything above except variant B is CSS.
