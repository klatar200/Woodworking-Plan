# Woodworking Plan — Independent UI/UX Audit (2026-07-21)

**Method.** Full source pulled into a clean environment and read component-by-component: `globals.css` (1,945 lines), `tailwind.css`, `ui.ts`, the root layout, every route in `src/app`, and every interactive component. Every claim below is verified against the shipped code, not the docs: contrast ratios computed from the actual token hex values, plan/category counts computed from `content/plans/*.json` (948 published / 167 unpublished / 6 categories), touch-target sizes read from the actual class strings, and the CAD-pilot containment confirmed by tracing imports. Where I could not run a real browser (Clerk-hosted flows, iOS zoom behaviour), I say so.

**Reviewer stance.** The twelve self-scored "95–97/100" sprints are treated as unverified. Short version of my verdict: the *architecture* genuinely earns high marks — the progressive-enhancement contract is intact everywhere I checked, and the amount of honest, decision-relevant copy is unusual. The *polish* scores are overstated: the app currently violates its own two loudest hard constraints (≥44px touch targets; a11y baseline including `aria-current` on nav) in multiple shipped components, and the light theme — the default — fails WCAG AA text contrast in several visible places while the "uncommissioned" dark theme passes comfortably.

## A. Documentation drift — the three reconciliations

### D1. Dark mode: the brief is flatly wrong, and the shipped feature is half-delivered

**What I found.** `DESIGN_BRIEF.md` §2 says "There is no dark mode by decision… don't add one uncommissioned." The code ships a complete, well-engineered dark theme (Sprint 31, explicitly Keagan-directed per the `globals.css` header): a `.dark {}` token flip covering every color, a *separate* dark elevation scale (inset top-edge highlights — the right call for dark UIs), print-safe resets that force light tokens on paper, `color-scheme` flips for native controls, and a dark skeleton shimmer. The mechanism is a `theme` cookie read **server-side** in `layout.tsx` and stamped onto `<html>` before paint — no flash of wrong theme. This is genuinely good work.

**The toggle.** It exists in exactly one place: inside the **account modal**, under "Preferences" — which is only reachable via the avatar, which only renders for **signed-in** users (`<SignedIn>` in `site-header.tsx`). Verified consequences:

- **Signed-out users cannot enable dark mode at all.** No toggle in the mobile drawer, footer, or anywhere public.
- **OS preference is ignored by design** (cookie, not `prefers-color-scheme`). Combined with the hidden toggle, a user whose phone is in dark mode gets a bright cream app in a dim workshop with no visible way to fix it. For *this* app's stated context — a phone in a shop, in bad light — that's not a nit.
- **Discoverability even for signed-in users is poor**: avatar → modal → scroll past Activity and the entire Workshop tool picker → "Preferences." Nobody will find it who isn't looking.

**Dark-mode quality, audited as a first-class surface (as requested).** I computed WCAG ratios for every token pair in use. The dark theme is *excellent*: worst text pair is `--muted-2` on `--surface` at **4.58:1** (passes AA); `--fg` on `--bg` is 15.6:1, `--muted` 7.3–8.1:1, all functional colors 5.7–8.3:1, checked-pill ink-on-orange 8.5:1. The irony is sharp: **the "uncommissioned" dark theme is more AA-compliant than the default light theme** (see A1).

Gaps where dark mode is *not* intentional:

- **Clerk surfaces are hard-coded light.** `src/lib/clerk-appearance.ts` supplies literal light hex values (`colorBackground: '#ffffff'`, cream inputs) with no dark variant, and the file's own comment says it must be updated by hand. In dark mode: the sign-in and sign-up pages, and the "Manage account & security" modal opened *from the dark account modal*, render as a white flashbang. This is the single most jarring dark-mode moment in the app.
- **Floating surfaces skip the dark elevation treatment** — see V1. The Browse dropdown, mobile drawer, overflow menu, and account modal use ad-hoc `shadow-[0_8px_24px_rgba(0,0,0,…)]` literals instead of `shadow-e*`, so they get none of the inset edge highlight the dark elevation tokens exist to provide; on `#221e17`-on-`#17140f` they read edge-less.
- **Browser chrome doesn't follow**: `themeColor` is fixed at `#1a1a1a` (viewport export) and the manifest says `background_color: #ffffff` — a dark status bar on the light theme, a white splash on the dark theme. Polish.

**What this means for anyone trusting the brief.** A reviewer applying the brief would file the entire dark theme as scope violation; an agent obeying it would refuse to maintain dark tokens (the exact class of bug the `globals.css` comments warn about — a token added to `:root` and forgotten in `.dark`). The brief must be updated to describe the cookie/class mechanism, the dual elevation scale, and the rule that every new color token needs a `.dark` counterpart.

**Recommendation.** (1) Update `DESIGN_BRIEF.md` §2 — quick win. (2) Put the toggle where everyone can reach it: the mobile drawer and the footer, available signed-out (it's a cookie; no account needed) — quick win. (3) Decide on `prefers-color-scheme` as the *default when no cookie exists* — this reverses a logged decision, so it's a product-owner call, but I'd argue for it given the use case. (4) Add a dark variant to `clerkAppearance` keyed off the same cookie. **Severity: high** (discoverability + Clerk flash), with the underlying theme itself in good shape.

### D2. Tailwind: the brief says "none"; the reality is a dual system, mostly disciplined, drifting at the edges

**What I found.** The brief says "plain CSS with design tokens… no Tailwind… keep it that way." Reality: Tailwind v4 is imported *first* in `layout.tsx`, deliberately layered (`theme`/`utilities` only, no preflight) so unlayered `globals.css` wins conflicts, and roughly half the component chrome (buttons, cards, header, filters, tabs, step walker, reviews, landing) now lives in utility strings — many with arbitrary values.

**Where the discipline holds.** Color is nearly airtight: `@theme inline` maps `bg-*`/`text-*`/`shadow-e*` utilities straight to `var(--token)`, so the dark flip re-themes utilities for free. That part of the migration is a model of how to do this.

**Where it leaks — concrete examples, as requested:**

- **Type scale.** The core system uses 0.75 / 0.8125 / 0.875 / 0.9375 / 1 / 1.0625rem. The landing page (`src/app/page.tsx`) mints its own: `text-[0.72rem]`, `[0.9rem]`, `[0.93rem]`, `[0.95rem]`, `[1.02rem]`, `[1.1rem]`, `[1.15rem]`. Three different "body small" sizes (0.93/0.95/1.02) coexist on one page, none matching the 0.9375 used for the same role everywhere else. Invisible individually; systemically it means "landing text size" is now unanswerable.
- **Radius scale.** Core: 0.375 / 0.5 / 0.75rem. Landing adds `rounded-[0.6rem]`, `[0.7rem]`, `[0.8rem]`, `[1.1rem]`. Four new radii on one page.
- **Shadows.** The QOL-F elevation tokens (`--elev-1/2/3`, exposed as `shadow-e1/e2/e3`) are bypassed by exactly the surfaces that float: `browsePanel` and the overflow menu use `shadow-[0_8px_24px_rgba(0,0,0,0.14)]`, the mobile drawer `[0_8px_24px_rgba(0,0,0,0.12)]`, the account modal `[0_16px_48px_rgba(0,0,0,0.28)]`, the filter drawer `[-8px_0_24px_rgba(0,0,0,0.18)]`. Five ad-hoc shadows, none warm-tinted (the elevation comments specifically argue grey shadows read as "dirt" on cream), none carrying the dark-mode inset highlight, none reset by the token-level print kill (the blanket `box-shadow: none` catches them — one more `!important` doing cleanup).
- **Cascade fights as a smell.** `compactOnMobile` (six `!important` utilities — and, verified, now *dead code*: zero call sites), footer headings needing `text-[0.75rem]!` to beat the global `h2`, `gap-0!` in the drawer, `font-medium!` in tabs. Each is individually justified in comments; collectively they're the tax of running an unlayered hand-written system against layered utilities indefinitely.

**What this means for anyone trusting the brief.** Worse than cosmetic: because `globals.css` is unlayered, a contributor who obeys the brief and writes new plain-CSS rules will silently *beat* existing utility styling and break converted components in ways no diff review of their CSS would reveal. The brief's styling section is actively dangerous to follow.

**Recommendation.** Update the brief to document the real system (tokens are the source of truth; utilities must reference them; arbitrary values only for pixel-parity migration, not new design). Then two cheap enforcement moves: add the landing's recurring one-offs as real tokens (or round them onto the scale), and replace the five ad-hoc popover shadows with `shadow-e2`/`shadow-e3`. Delete `compactOnMobile`. **Severity: medium**, structural.

### D3. Typography: Fraunces scoping is clean; the inconsistency worth fixing is elsewhere

**What I found.** The brief says "system-ui stack," full stop. Reality: Fraunces is loaded via `next/font` (self-hosted, no CSP hole), exposed as `--font-display`, and applied via `.font-display` — which, verified by grep, is used **only in `src/app/page.tsx`** (landing headings, step numbers, card titles on landing). No leakage onto any other page; the landing's *body* copy stays system-ui, so serif display over sans body reads as a deliberate marketing/brand pairing. That part is fine and I'd call it intentional hierarchy, not inconsistency.

**The actual typographic seam** is between the landing and the app. The landing's `h2`s are Fraunces at `clamp(1.5rem, 3vw, 2rem)`; every app page's `h2` is the global rule: **1rem, uppercase, letterspaced, muted** — an eyebrow label, not a heading — under an `h1` of only 1.5rem (1.75rem on plan detail). One click from the confident landing hero into `/browse` or `/saved` and the type hierarchy visibly flattens: page titles barely larger than body text, section "headings" that read as form labels. It's consistent *within* the app, so it's defensible, but it's the main reason the app pages feel like an earlier design generation than the landing (see gut check).

**What this means for anyone trusting the brief.** Minor but real: an agent told "system-ui, full stop" might strip the `fraunces.variable` from `<html>` as cleanup, killing the landing's brand element. One sentence in the brief fixes it.

**Recommendation.** Update the brief ("system-ui for UI; Fraunces via `--font-display` scoped to marketing headings — extendable to site-wide h1s if commissioned"). Separately, consider promoting `h1` to ~1.875–2rem on app pages and giving real section headings a 1.125–1.25rem non-uppercase treatment, keeping the eyebrow style for actual eyebrows. **Severity: low-medium** (brief fix is trivial; the hierarchy change is a design decision).

## B. Heuristic & IA findings

### H1. Deleting a review is one tap, irreversible, and quietly destroys more than the review — **High**

**What's wrong.** The "Delete" button on your own review (`reviews-section.tsx`, `deleteReviewAction`) fires immediately: no confirmation, no undo. Because the data model derives *builds* and *learning-path progress* from reviews ("reviewed ⇒ built," per `builds.ts`/`paths.ts`), deleting a review also removes that build from `/builds` and un-ticks the corresponding path step — a consequence nothing in the UI mentions. Photo "Remove" is likewise instant.

**Where.** `/plans/[slug]`, reviews section, signed-in as the author (or admin); any width.

**Why it matters.** This is the user's only long-lived personal record in the app ("your review is the record," the paths page says). A mis-tap on a phone — the primary device — erases a build log entry, its photos, and path progress with zero friction and zero recovery. Nielsen: error prevention + user control both violated at once, on the most valuable data the user owns.

**Recommendation.** Keep it no-JS-safe with a server-rendered two-step: "Delete" navigates with `?confirm=<reviewId>`, page re-renders that review with "This removes your review, photos, and this plan's entry in Your builds — Delete / Keep." (Or a `<details>`-wrapped confirm, zero navigation.) Add the consequence sentence either way. **Quick win.**

### H2. The shopping list can't shed a plan from the shopping list — **Medium-high**

**What's wrong.** Removal lives only in each plan page's "…" overflow menu ("On shopping list" toggle). `/shopping-list` itself offers no per-plan remove — not even in the by-plan view where each plan has its own section.

**Where.** `/shopping-list`, both views; the gap bites hardest on mobile in a store.

**Why it matters.** The store scenario is the page's whole reason to exist: you're at the lumberyard, decide to drop one of three projects, and the only path is shopping-list → plan page → "…" menu → toggle → back — per plan, on one bar of signal. Recognition over recall inverted: the list shows the aggregation but hides the membership control.

**Recommendation.** In the by-plan view, a small "Remove from list" form per plan section posting the existing `removeFromShoppingListAction` (it already takes `planId` + `returnTo`); in the merged view, list the contributing plans with remove affordances in a footer block. Pure server-action forms — no new architecture. **Quick win.**

### H3. The step walker forgets where you were, and "Next" doesn't move the viewport — **Medium-high**

**What's wrong.** `step-walker.tsx` keeps the active step in component state only. Refresh, accidental back-swipe, phone sleep + browser eviction, or reopening offline → back to step 1 with no memory. And `goTo()` only sets state: tapping **Next** at the *bottom* of a long step leaves you scrolled past the top of the new step's content — the next thing you see is whatever happens to be at that scroll offset, and you scroll up to find the step you just advanced to.

**Where.** `/plans/[slug]/build`, mobile especially (dots + Prev/Next), mid-build.

**Why it matters.** This is *the* workshop surface — dusty gloves, phone propped on a vise, screen sleeping between cuts. Step 9 of 14, phone relocks, tab reloads: you're rederiving your place by memory. The scroll gap makes every Next on a long step a two-gesture operation.

**Recommendation.** Persist `{slug: activeStep}` in `localStorage` and restore on mount (pure enhancement — no-JS/print contract untouched); on step change, `scrollIntoView` the step container (respecting `prefers-reduced-motion`). Consider a sticky bottom Prev/Next bar on mobile so the advance control is always in thumb reach at a fixed position. Not a quick win, but a contained one.

### H5. Pre-ticked-but-inactive tool filters; one query per checkbox tick — **Medium**

**What's wrong.** Two related honesty problems in the filter panel. (1) For a signed-in user with a saved workshop, the "Tools you own" boxes render **checked** while the results are **unfiltered** — control state disagrees with system state, patched by a sentence of explainer text ("Pre-filled from your workshop — adjust any filter to apply"). (2) Auto-apply debounces at 200ms — shorter than any human inter-tap interval — so ticking five tools fires five soft navigations and five catalog queries, with results, count, and chips churning behind the still-open drawer.

**Where.** `/browse`, filter drawer (mobile) / rail (desktop), signed-in with a workshop; the multi-tick case is any tools/difficulty/cost multi-select.

**Why it matters.** Visibility of system status: checked-but-inactive is precisely the state a checkbox exists to not have; users will believe they're seeing filtered results when they aren't. The per-tick navigation is wasteful (server round-trips) and visually noisy on the gloved multi-select flow the tool filter is *for*.

**Recommendation.** For (1): render prefill as a suggestion, not state — an unticked panel plus the existing "Show plans I can build" CTA (which already does this job well), or tick *and apply* on load via redirect. For (2): raise the debounce to ~600–800ms, or batch until drawer close on mobile. The pre-fill decision is a small product call; the debounce is a one-number change.

### H6. The installed app opens to marketing — **Medium, quick win**

**What's wrong.** `manifest.webmanifest` has `start_url: "/"` — which, since QOL-M, is the marketing landing. Every cold launch of the installed PWA opens hero copy, marquees, and a "Create a free account" CTA aimed at the person who has already installed the app.

**Where.** PWA launch, standalone display mode.

**Why it matters.** The install pitch is "faster access, works offline in the shop." The shop user launches the app to reach a cut list; the landing is pure toll. It also burns the offline budget: the landing is `force-dynamic` and data-driven, so an offline cold launch lands on the marketing page's cached shell rather than the far more useful catalog.

**Recommendation.** `start_url: "/browse"` (keep `scope: "/"`). One line; verify the SW pre-caches `/browse`.

### H11. Mobile has no search outside the catalog page — **Medium, quick win**

**What's wrong.** `HeaderSearch` is `hidden lg:flex` — desktop only — and the mobile drawer contains no search field. On a phone, from a plan page, a path, or the FAQ, finding a specific plan requires knowing that search lives only on `/browse` and navigating there first.

**Where.** Header/drawer, any page except `/browse`, <64rem.

**Why it matters.** For a mobile-first catalog of ~950 items, search is a primary task, and it's the one primary task with no mobile entry point in the persistent chrome. "Reach is everywhere the header is" — the code comment's own justification for HeaderSearch — currently applies only to desktop.

**Recommendation.** Add the same plain GET form at the top of the mobile drawer (44px input, 16px font per house rules). No new architecture — it's the existing `role="search"` form with drawer styling.

### H4. Account modal vs `/profile`: coherent as primary/fallback, with one duplication worth watching — **Low-medium**

**What I found (evaluating the question you asked).** The split is more coherent than it first looks: the avatar is a real `<a href="/profile">` that JS upgrades into the modal, so `/profile` is formally the no-JS fallback, and credentials stay with Clerk in both worlds. The incoherence is that the **Workshop tool picker is fully implemented twice** — a client-side fetch/save version in the modal (`account-modal.tsx`, `/api/workshop` + `saveWorkshopModalAction`) and a server-form version on `/profile#workshop` (`workshop-form.tsx`) — with different save flows ("Saved" inline vs redirect + banner) and copy that must now be maintained in lockstep. Plan pages deep-link to `/profile#workshop`, so JS users *do* land on the fallback surface routinely, meaning both really are user-facing.

**Why it matters.** Two editing surfaces for one dataset is exactly the drift pattern this codebase's own comments warn about (it deleted `SaveButton` for this reason). Users who learn the modal version get a differently-shaped page from the plan-page link.

**Recommendation.** Pick one canonical picker: either the plan page's "Update your workshop" opens the account modal when JS is present (falling back to `/profile#workshop`), or the modal's Workshop section becomes a link to `/profile#workshop`. Either collapses the duplication without touching the no-JS path.

### Smaller heuristic notes

- **Sort control vanishes during search** (`SortSelect` returns null) with no visible explanation. The reasoning (relevance is the sort) is sound; a disabled select labelled "Sorted by relevance" would preserve visibility of system status. **Low.**
- **Collection delete has no confirm** (`/saved`). Mitigated well: the inline caption "The plans stay saved — only the collection is removed" is exactly the right disclosure, and the loss is only the grouping. Acceptable as-is. **Polish.**
- **Overflow menu doesn't close on outside click** (documented trade for zero hydration). Both items navigate away; acceptable. **Polish.**
- **No-JS ARIA orphan:** the plan-detail panels server-render `role="tabpanel" aria-labelledby="tab-*"` even before/without the tablist existing, so no-JS/pre-hydration ARIA references point at nothing. Harmless in practice, but the roles should arguably be added by the same JS that adds the tablist. **Polish.**

## C. Visual & interaction consistency

### V1. The floating layer ignores the elevation system — **Medium, quick win**

**What's wrong.** The five surfaces that most need depth — Browse dropdown, mobile drawer, plan-page overflow menu, account modal, mobile filter drawer — each carry a hand-written grey `shadow-[…]` literal instead of `shadow-e2/e3` (details in D2). In light mode they're subtly colder than the warm-tinted card shadows around them; in dark mode they miss the inset edge highlight that the dark elevation tokens exist to provide, so panels barely separate from the page.

**Where.** `site-header.tsx` (`browsePanel`), `mobile-nav.tsx`, `overflow-menu.tsx`, `account-modal.tsx`, `filter-disclosure.tsx`.

**Why it matters.** The QOL-F elevation pass's own documentation makes the argument: one token per level or components drift. These five drifted, and they're the components a "modern SaaS depth" pass is judged by.

**Recommendation.** Swap literals for `shadow-e3` (modal, drawers) and `shadow-e2` (menus); if a directional shadow is wanted for the side drawer, mint it as a token pair with a dark variant.

### V2. The landing is its own micro design system — **Medium**

Covered concretely in D2 (type/radius one-offs). The interaction layer adds two more: the landing's FAQ accordion has **no chevron affordance and no animation** while `/faq`'s has both (rotating chevron, height transition) — two accordion dialects one click apart; and the landing's card-hover lift (`-translate-y-[4px]`/`[6px]`, `shadow-e3`) is shared with catalog cards (good) but the "how it works" cards lift 6px vs. every other card's 4px. **Recommendation:** reuse the `/faq` summary treatment on the landing, and standardize the lift at 4px.

### V3. Pagination is a different visual family — **Low**

`pagination-number` uses heavy ink borders (`border: 1px solid var(--fg)`, bold) where every neighboring control uses soft `--border` outlines; the active state is solid ink while every other "active" in the app (chips, tabs, rail) is orange `--accent`. It reads like a Sprint-3 survivor next to the QOL-era chrome around it. Also 38px tall — see M1. **Recommendation:** restyle as chips (border-border resting, `chipActive` orange for the current page) at ≥44px.

### V4. Compressed app-page type hierarchy — **Low-medium**

See D3 — the global 1rem-uppercase `h2` and 1.5rem `h1` flatten every app page. Structural, not a bug; noted here because it's the single biggest "different design pass" signal between landing and app.

## D. Mobile ergonomics (the gloves test)

### M1. The 44px hard constraint is violated by shipped controls — **High, mostly quick wins**

The constraint is real in `btnBase`/inputs/selects (2.75rem = 44px, consistently). But measured from the shipped class strings, these are below it:

| Control | Size | File |
|---|---|---|
| **Step dots — the primary mobile step nav** | 32px (`w-[2rem] h-[2rem]`) | `step-walker.tsx` |
| Mobile filter trigger pill | 36px (`min-h-[2.25rem]`), 13px text | `filter-disclosure.tsx` |
| Filter drawer close ✕ | 36px | `filter-disclosure.tsx` |
| Account avatar (only path to account/theme) | 36px | `account-menu.tsx` |
| Account modal close ✕ | 36px | `account-modal.tsx` |
| Pagination numbers | 38px (`min-*-[2.375rem]`) | `globals.css` |
| Checkbox pills / chips (filters, collections, view toggles) | 40px (`min-h-[2.5rem]`) | `ui.ts` |
| Collection-remove ✕ on saved badges | ~20px glyph, no min target | `saved/page.tsx` |
| Shopping-list checkbox | 18.4px (label is large, but the box is the tick affordance) | `shopping-list/page.tsx` |
| Breadcrumb back-pill | 36px (`min-height: 2.25rem`) | `globals.css` |

**Why it matters.** The 44px rule isn't a style preference here — it's the load-bearing answer to "gloves, sawdust, one thumb." The worst offender is the step dots: 32px targets with 8px gaps are the mid-build navigation, used at arm's length with work gloves. The filter trigger/close pair means the whole filter flow on mobile is bracketed by sub-standard targets. Several of these (filter pill, breadcrumb) are *documented* as deliberate shrinks — trading the app's number-one ergonomic rule for density on exactly the screens where the rule matters.

**Recommendation.** Dots → 44px (or replace dots with a scrubbable "Step N of M" stepper); filter trigger/close, avatar, modal close → `min-h/w-[2.75rem]`; pagination → 44px chips (V3); give the saved-badge ✕ a 44px hit area via padding; shopping-list checkbox → 24px box inside a 44px row (the row-as-label already helps). Almost all are class-string edits.

### M2. The filter drawer's keyboard/scroll manners — **Medium**

No Esc-to-close (native `<details>` doesn't provide it; the scrim and ✕ are the only outs), no focus containment (tab order walks out of the drawer into the page behind the scrim), and the page behind can still scroll on overscroll. Fine for a `<details>`-first architecture, but since a client component is already present, an `onKeyDown` Esc handler and `inert` on the page background while open are cheap upgrades.

### M3. Prev/Next position wanders — **Medium**

Covered in H3: the step nav buttons sit below content of arbitrary length, so the thumb target moves every step. A sticky bottom bar (mobile only, hidden in print — the `step-walker-nav` class is already print-hidden) would make advancing a fixed-position, eyes-free gesture.

### M4. Input font sizes — verified good, one edge

Every mobile-reachable input is 16px (search, selects, textarea, review fields) — the iOS-zoom rule is genuinely held. The desktop-only header search input is 15px (`text-[0.9375rem]`), which can zoom on an iPad wide enough to hit `lg`. **Polish.**

## E. Accessibility (verified, not assumed)

**What holds up — worth saying plainly.** Skip link: present, correctly revealed on focus, targets `id="main"` on every page including print/boards/offline (verified by grep). Heading order: correct everywhere I traced, including the deliberate visually-hidden "Results" h2 on `/browse` and the paths-page h2/h3 fix. Focus rings: consistent 2px `--ok` outline across every interactive class string I read (4.76:1 against cream — passes the 3:1 non-text requirement). Form labels: everywhere, including visually-hidden ones on searches and selects. The tabs implement the full WAI-ARIA roving-tabindex pattern; the star input is real radios with labels; the rating display carries a text equivalent; marquee duplicates are `inert` + `aria-hidden`. This is far above baseline for an app this age.

### A1. The default (light) theme fails AA text contrast — **High, quick win**

Computed from the shipped tokens:

| Pair | Ratio | AA (4.5:1 normal text) | Where it's used as text |
|---|---|---|---|
| `--accent-strong` #c96a2e on `--bg` #faf9f6 | **3.57:1** | ✗ | "Clear search and filters" link (`/browse` subtitle, 16px); landing eyebrows (11.5px semibold); hero "actually" |
| `--accent-strong` on `--surface` #fff | **3.76:1** | ✗ | star-rating glyph color (graphical — 3:1 — passes); tool-fit links |
| `--muted-2` #8a8175 on `--bg` | **3.64:1** | ✗ | landing trust marquee (15.2px), hero footnote (14.4px), step-rail label (12px) |
| `--muted-2` on `--surface` | **3.83:1** | ✗ | same contexts on surface bands |
| `--muted` #6b6b6b on bg/surface | 5.06–5.33:1 | ✓ | (fine — the workhorse muted is compliant) |

Meanwhile every dark-theme text pair passes (worst 4.58:1). The brief's "aimed at WCAG AA" claim is currently truer of the theme it says doesn't exist.

**Recommendation.** Mint a text-safe accent (≈`#b25a20` gets ~4.5:1 on cream) and use it wherever `accent-strong` is text at body size; keep `#c96a2e` for large text and glyphs. Darken `--muted-2` toward ≈`#75705f` (~4.6:1) or stop using it below 18px. Token edits only. Also: `--muted-2` isn't in the brief's token list at all — add it when updating (D1/D2).

### A2. No current-page indication in the header nav — **High, quick win**

Verified: `aria-current` appears in pagination, category rail, saved chips, shopping-list views, print chips, and the step walker — but **nowhere in `site-header.tsx` or `mobile-nav.tsx`**, and there's no visual active state either: on `/paths`, "Learning" looks identical to "About." The brief's claimed baseline ("`aria-current` on active nav/sort") is not true of the shipped header — the nav redesign regressed it (and the sort became a `<select>`, where the claim no longer applies; the brief should say so).

**Recommendation.** The header is a server component with access to nothing route-specific — the cheapest correct fix is a tiny client wrapper (or `usePathname` in `MobileNav`/a nav island, which already exists for the drawer) applying `aria-current="page"` + an ink-text/underline active style to the matching link. Update the brief's baseline list to match reality either way.

### A3. Marquees: unpausable on touch, focusable while moving — **Medium**

Three infinite auto-scrolling rows on the landing. Pause exists on `:hover` (unavailable on touch) and under `prefers-reduced-motion` (an OS-level setting most users never find) — WCAG 2.2.2 wants a user-reachable pause for motion >5s. And the featured marquee's copy-0 cards are *real, focusable* links moving at speed: keyboard focus lands on a drifting target whose ring slides toward the viewport edge, and Safari's scroll-to-focus fights the animation.

**Recommendation.** One CSS line adds most of the fix: pause the track on `:focus-within` as well as hover. For touch, either a small pause toggle per band or the simpler product answer — below `lg`, render the featured plans as a static horizontally scrollable row (swipe is the native marquee).

### A4. Invalid checkbox `id`s on the shopping list — **Medium, quick win**

`id={`have-${line.name}`}` interpolates raw material names — "have-Wood glue" contains a space (invalid HTML `id`), and the same material appearing in two unit groups yields duplicate ids. Label association (the *large* target that compensates for the small box — M1) becomes unreliable exactly where it matters. **Recommendation:** slugify and namespace: `have-${group.unit}-${slugify(name)}-${species ?? ''}`.

### A5. Silent result updates on auto-applied filters — **Medium, quick win**

With soft navigation, ticking a filter re-renders the results with no announcement: the count paragraph isn't a live region, and the route announcer has no title change to speak on a same-path query update. A screen-reader user who toggles "Beginner" hears nothing. **Recommendation:** `aria-live="polite"` (or `role="status"`) on the results-count paragraph in `/browse` — it already re-renders with the right sentence ("42 plans match your filters").

## Gut check

To a first-time visitor this reads as a real product, and a strangely trustworthy one: the landing is confident and current, the data-forward cards deliver on the "actually compare" promise immediately, and the app's habit of telling the truth — cost bands with a stated reason, "Photo coming soon," a contact line that admits it's a placeholder, waste percentages on the board plan — builds more credibility than most polished catalogs manage. It does not read as fully *finished*: the seam between the expressive landing and the flat-hierarchy, eyebrow-headed app pages feels like two design generations, placeholder iconography follows you to the home screen, and small-control ergonomics (tiny dots, 36px chrome) whisper "engineer-polished, not designer-finished." Nothing here is structurally wrong — the bones are unusually good — but the distance between "96/100" and reality is exactly the list above: contrast, targets, nav state, reachability of the dark theme, and mercy for the user's own data.
